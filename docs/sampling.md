# Sample-space reduction summary

## Starting point

The original `RobustInterceptConfigGenerator` exposes 16 sampling parameters (range, two camera angles, two image-plane coords, camera roll, two target-velocity params, target speed, three body rates, three wind params, forward speed). For the scenario class under study, most are fixed by config, leaving **4 active sampling dimensions**:

$$\Theta_{\text{full}} = (\alpha,\, \varepsilon,\, u,\, v) \in [0°, 360°) \times [-90°, 90°] \times [-0.9, 0.9]^2$$

where $\alpha$ = `camera_azimuth_deg`, $\varepsilon$ = `camera_elevation_deg`, $(u, v)$ = `camera_u_fraction`, `camera_v_fraction`. All other parameters (range = 8m, forward speed = 2 m/s, target speed = 0, wind = 0, body rates = 0) are held constant.

## The reduction

**Final dimensions: 3.** The collapse is $4D \to 3D$ by fixing $\alpha = 0$.

$$\Theta_{\text{collapsed}} = (\varepsilon,\, u,\, v) \in [-90°, 90°] \times [-0.9, 0.9]^2$$

The flattened dimension is `camera_azimuth_deg`. The retained dimensions are `camera_elevation_deg`, `camera_u_fraction`, `camera_v_fraction`.

## Mathematical justification

The simulator dynamics are invariant under any rotation $R \in G$ where $G$ is the symmetry group of all external forces and constraints. For this scenario:

- No wind, no body-frame asymmetries in the controller relative to world → world-frame rotational symmetries are determined entirely by gravity.
- Gravity $\vec{g} = -g\hat{z}$ is invariant under rotations about $\hat{z}$.

Therefore $G = SO(2)_z$ — the group of yaw rotations about the world vertical axis.

For any sample $\theta = (\alpha, \varepsilon, u, v)$ and any $R_\phi \in SO(2)_z$, the trajectory generated by $\theta$ and the trajectory generated by $R_\phi \cdot \theta = (\alpha + \phi, \varepsilon, u, v)$ are related by $R_\phi$ itself. That is, they're the same engagement viewed from a yaw-rotated world frame.

Formally, let $\Phi_t(\theta)$ denote the simulator state at time $t$ starting from initial condition $\theta$. Then:

$$\Phi_t((\alpha + \phi, \varepsilon, u, v)) = R_\phi \cdot \Phi_t((\alpha, \varepsilon, u, v))$$

Outcome metrics (catch / no-catch, miss distance, time-to-intercept) are scalar invariants under this rotation, so:

$$M(\Phi_T(\alpha, \varepsilon, u, v)) = M(\Phi_T(0, \varepsilon, u, v))$$

for any catch metric $M$. The azimuth dimension contributes no new information to outcome distributions and can be quotiented out.

**Empirical verification:** an 8-point azimuth sweep at fixed $(\varepsilon, u, v)$ on two distinct policies showed sub-millimeter trajectory deviation (worst case $1.4 \times 10^{-4}$ m on an 8m engagement, ratio $\sim 10^{-5}$) after applying the inverse yaw rotation. Within float32 integration noise.

## Why $\varepsilon$, $u$, $v$ cannot be similarly collapsed

Each remaining dimension breaks a different symmetry that gravity preserves only along its own axis:

- $\varepsilon$ controls the angle between the pursuer's velocity and gravity. Climbing, level, and diving engagements have qualitatively different trajectory shapes under gravitational load.
- $u$ offsets the LOS horizontally relative to the camera bore-sight; the resulting initial perpendicular LOS rate must be compensated by lateral acceleration that competes with gravity-induced sag.
- $v$ offsets the LOS vertically; this combines additively with $\varepsilon$ to set the total LOS elevation in the world frame, and so directly couples to gravity in a way distinct from $u$.

Verification probes confirmed that varying any of these three produces meaningfully different trajectory outcomes (min-distance, final pursuer position) — they are not redundant.

## Geometric description

**Original 4D space.** The full parameter space is a product of a sphere and a disk: $S^2 \times D^2$. The $S^2$ factor is the unit sphere of possible pursuer velocity directions in world frame, coordinatized by $(\alpha, \varepsilon)$. The $D^2$ factor is the camera image plane (rectangular, but interchangeable with a disk for this discussion), coordinatized by $(u, v)$ — where the target appears in the pursuer's image. Equivalently: a pursuer can fly in any 3D direction toward any in-FOV target position, with both choices independent.

**Symmetry to quotient.** The group $SO(2)_z$ acts on this space by yaw-rotating the velocity sphere (rotating $\alpha$) while leaving the FOV disk untouched (the camera is rigidly attached to the pursuer; image-plane coordinates don't care about world yaw). The orbits of this action are circles of constant latitude on $S^2$, swept at each fixed point of $D^2$.

**Quotient space.** $S^2 / SO(2)_z$ is a 1D arc — the set of distinct latitudes on the sphere, i.e., elevation values from $-90°$ to $+90°$. The collapsed space is thus:

$$(S^2 \times D^2) / SO(2)_z \;=\; [\,-90°, +90°\,] \times D^2$$

**Geometric interpretation of the collapsed space.** Picture a half-meridian on the velocity sphere — a 1D curved line from south pole through equator to north pole, lying in the world $xz$-plane. At each point of this meridian, attach a 2D FOV image plane indicating where the target can appear. The full sample space is the resulting 3D fiber bundle: a 1D backbone of velocity directions, fibered over by 2D image planes.

**What sample points actually look like in world coordinates.** Each $(\varepsilon, u, v)$ sample determines a unit LOS vector $\hat{r}(\varepsilon, u, v)$, and the pursuer position is then $-r_0 \hat{r}$ from the target. As $(\varepsilon, u, v)$ vary, the pursuer position sweeps out a 2D patch on the radius-$r_0$ sphere around the target. This patch lies on a band roughly centered on the great circle in the $xz$-plane, with thickness in the $y$-direction set by the horizontal FOV half-angle. The pursuer's velocity at each point of this patch is determined by $\varepsilon$ alone (independent of $u, v$); the velocity rotates smoothly as the position sweeps along the elevation direction of the patch.

The 3D bundle is not the same as the 2D position patch on the sphere — the bundle has an extra dimension corresponding to the fact that, at any given pursuer position reachable through multiple $(\varepsilon, u, v)$ combinations, the velocity direction depends on which combination produced that position. Position and velocity are not separately specified; they're jointly determined by the three angles.

## Sampling budget implication

A 7200-sample Sobol sequence over the original 4D cube yields $\sim 7200^{1/4} \approx 9.2$ effective resolution per dimension. The same budget applied to the 3D collapsed cube yields $\sim 7200^{1/3} \approx 19.3$ per dimension — a 2.1× density improvement along each axis that actually drives outcomes. Equivalently, $\sim 660$ samples in 3D match the per-dimension density of 7200 samples in 4D, an 11× budget reduction at fixed coverage.

## Scope of validity

The reduction is exact for the current `SimEngine` configuration: gravity along $-\hat{z}$, no wind, no other world-frame asymmetric forces. Adding any world-frame vector field not aligned with $\hat{z}$ (horizontal wind, magnetic field, north-aligned sensor bias, etc.) breaks $SO(2)_z$ and would require restoring `camera_azimuth_deg` as an active dimension.
