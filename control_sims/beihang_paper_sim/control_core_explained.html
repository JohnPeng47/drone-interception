Objective 1 — bearing
$1 - n_{td}^\top n_t < \epsilon$
Keep the designed LOS aligned with the actual LOS. A purely geometric goal — doesn't care about range or velocity.
Objective 2 — closing range
$p_r \to 0$
Collapse the relative position. A dynamical goal — requires shaping acceleration via thrust direction.

Piece 1 · LOS unit vectors 1

Piece 2 · Velocity error 4
$z_2 = v_r - v_{rd},\;\; v_{rd} = -k_1 p_r$
Why does $v_{rd} = -k_1 p_r$ close range?

Take $L_2 = \tfrac{1}{2} p_r^\top p_r$. Then $\dot L_2 = p_r^\top v_r$.

Choose $v_{rd} = -k_1 p_r$. When $v_r$ tracks $v_{rd}$ exactly, $\dot L_2 = -k_1\|p_r\|^2 \le 0$, so $\|p_r\|$ decays exponentially.

Direction check: $-k_1 p_r = k_1 \|p_r\|\,n_t$ — magnitude proportional to range, aimed along the LOS toward the target.

Piece 1 · Barrier error 2
$z_1 = 1 - n_{td}^\top n_t,\quad L_1 = \tfrac{1}{2}\log\!\dfrac{k_b^2}{k_b^2 - z_1^2}$
The barrier — what keeps the target on-screen

As $|z_1| \to k_b$, $L_1 \to \infty$. Any finite control effort can't push the system past the barrier, so the FOV constraint is enforced automatically — provided $z_1(0) < k_b$.

Derivative: $\dot L_1 = \dfrac{z_1}{k_b^2 - z_1^2}\,\dot z_1$. The barrier prefactor $z_1/(k_b^2 - z_1^2)$ is what appears in $b\omega_1$.

Piece 2 · Desired acceleration 5
$a_d = -k_1 v_r - k_2 z_2 - p_r + \dfrac{m}{\lVert p_r\rVert}\,\dfrac{z_1}{k_b^2 - z_1^2}\,(\text{proj})\,n_{td}$
Where each term comes from

$-k_2 z_2$ — drives velocity-tracking error to zero (sets Lyapunov decrement $-k_2\|z_2\|^2$).

$-k_1 v_r - p_r$ — terms inherited from $\dot v_{rd}$ and the $\dot L_2$ cross-term.

Last term — bearing/range coupling. It cancels the $\dot L_1$ cross-coupling so Pieces 1 and 2 superpose cleanly (Appendix A).

Piece 2 · Desired thrust direction (Eq. 21) 6

What is $R_{\text{tilt}}$?

The minimum-angle rotation that maps the current body-z $n_f$ onto the desired thrust direction $n_{fd}$. Built by Rodrigues' formula in _tilt_rotation using axis $n_f \times n_{fd}$.

Piece 2 · Body-rate command (Eq. 26) 8
$b\omega_2 = -\mathrm{vex}(R_d^\top R_{wb} - R_{wb}^\top R_d)$
Superposition + saturation (Eq. 28) 9
$b\omega_d = \mathrm{sat}\big(b\omega_1 + b\omega_2,\;\omega_m\big)$
Why is the sum stable?

Appendix A shows the combined Lyapunov $L = L_1 + L_2 + \tfrac{1}{2}\|z_2\|^2$ has cross-terms that exactly cancel due to the last term in $a_d$. The result:

$\dot L_3 = -\dfrac{z_1}{k_b^2 - z_1^2}(n_{td}\times n_t)^\top {}^e\omega - k_1\|p_r\|^2 - k_2\|z_2\|^2$.

Three negative-definite terms. Each piece "pays for itself" in its own slot of the Lyapunov.

Thrust magnitude (Eq. 23) 10
$f_d = \mathrm{clip}\big(n_f^\top (m\,a_d - m g - e_f^{\text{drag}}),\;0,\;f_{\max}\big)$
Output · CtbrCommand
$\big(t,\; f_d,\; b\omega_d\big)$

The structural insight. Both pieces emit body-rate commands because $b\omega$ is the only currency available on an underactuated multirotor — the body has to rotate for thrust to point anywhere except $+z_b$. But the reasons are unrelated: Piece 1 rotates the body to aim the camera; Piece 2 rotates the body to aim the thrust vector so that closing velocity tracks $v_{rd} = -k_1 p_r$. The Lyapunov analysis shows the two demands superpose cleanly.

Walkthrough

0Observer state in
Three quantities arrive each tick: relative position $p_r$, relative velocity $v_r$, and the vehicle attitude $R_{wb}$. Everything downstream is computed from these — the camera direction never appears as a raw measurement, only as n_t derived from $p_r$.
1LOS unit vectors — the geometric inputs
$n_t$ is the actual LOS (interceptor → target); $n_{td}$ is the designed LOS (camera optical axis, body-z, rotated into world). Piece 1's entire job is to make these two align. Both are directions, no magnitudes, no derivatives — that's why target acceleration $a_t$ cannot directly perturb this loop.
2Barrier error $z_1$ and Lyapunov $L_1$
$z_1 \in [0, 2]$ measures misalignment: $0$ when perfectly pointed, $2$ when antipodal. $L_1$ is a log-barrier — finite for $|z_1| < k_b$, infinite at the boundary. This is what guarantees the target never leaves the FOV cone, structurally, not just on average.
3$b\omega_1$ — the bearing body-rate (Eq. 13)
Pure kinematic servoing. The cross product $n_{td} \times n_t$ gives an axis in the world frame; $R_{wb}^\top$ pulls it into the body frame; the barrier prefactor scales aggressively as misalignment grows. This loop is immune to $a_t$ — $\dot z_1$ depends on $v_r$ and $\,{}^e\omega$ only, never on target acceleration.
4Velocity error $z_2$ — the closing-velocity error
In code: z_2 = v_r + k_1 * p_r. That's $z_2 = v_r - v_{rd}$ with $v_{rd} = -k_1 p_r$ — the desired closing velocity, proportional to range and pointed along the LOS toward the target. Everything downstream is "make $v_r$ track $v_{rd}$".
5$a_d$ — desired acceleration
Four terms: $-k_2 z_2$ drives velocity tracking; $-k_1 v_r - p_r$ inherit from $\dot v_{rd}$; the final barrier-weighted term cancels the cross-coupling between Piece 1 and Piece 2 in the Lyapunov derivative. Under target maneuvering, an unmodeled $-a_t$ term shows up here — that's how $\|a_t\|$ enters the closing loop but not the bearing loop.
6$n_{fd}$ — desired thrust direction
Invert the dynamics: to produce acceleration $a_d$ you need thrust direction $n_{fd} \propto a_d - g - \text{drag}$. This is the kinematic-to-attitude conversion that any underactuated thrust-vectoring vehicle must do.
7$R_d$ — desired attitude
Apply the minimum-angle rotation that takes current body-z $n_f$ to desired thrust direction $n_{fd}$, composed with current attitude. Roll/yaw about thrust axis are free degrees — Piece 1 uses them for camera pointing.
8$b\omega_2$ — closing-velocity body-rate (Eq. 26)
The body-rate that drives current attitude toward $R_d$. Note: the purpose here is closing velocity, not attitude — attitude is an intermediate variable. The chain is $v_{rd} \to z_2 \to a_d \to n_{fd} \to R_d \to b\omega_2$, four stages of inversion.
9Superposition + saturation
$b\omega_1$ and $b\omega_2$ are two demands on the same actuator. The Lyapunov design (last term of $a_d$) makes the cross-terms cancel. Saturation at $\omega_m$ is where the practical FOV-tracking limit lives: if LOS rate exceeds $\omega_m$, Piece 1's command gets clipped and the target can drift out of frame — independent of Piece 2's health.
10Thrust magnitude $f_d$
Project the required force $m a_d - mg - \text{drag}$ onto the current body-z to get a scalar thrust. Clipped to $[0, f_{\max}]$. When saturated, $a_d$ can't be realized and $z_2$ grows.
The asymmetry that matters. Piece 1 is kinematic (directions, angular rates). Piece 2 is dynamic (accelerations). Target maneuvering is a dynamical disturbance — it hits Piece 2 and spares Piece 1. The benign failure mode: target stays centered on screen, but interceptor arrives with biased terminal velocity. The bad failure mode (target leaves frame) is gated by saturation on $b\omega_1$, a separate threshold.
