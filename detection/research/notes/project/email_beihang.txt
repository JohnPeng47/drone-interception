Subject:  Question on detector choice in your IBVS interception work

Dear Prof. Li, Dr. Yang, and Mr. Yan,

I have been reading your recent papers on monocular IBVS interception
("High-Speed Interception Multicopter Control by Image-based Visual
Servoing", arXiv:2404.08296, IEEE TCST 2025; and "Precise Interception
Flight Targets by Image-based Visual Servoing of Multicopter",
arXiv:2409.17497, IEEE TIE 2025) and have a question I hope you might
be willing to clarify.

In the outdoor flight experiments, the intruder is a tethered red
balloon, and the perception module is described as YOLOv7 without
further detail on training. I assume the balloon was chosen partly to
isolate the controller as the contribution and partly for safety and
repeatability. My question is whether you considered, or have results
from, training the detector on synthetic images of an actual drone
target rather than relying on a high-contrast balloon proxy.

There is a small but growing body of work showing that CNN drone
detectors trained on rendered/synthetic imagery transfer reasonably
well to real flight footage -- e.g., Barisic et al. (Sim2Air, RA-L
2022, arXiv:2110.05145), Lenhard et al. (SynDroneVision, WACV 2025,
arXiv:2411.05633), and Wisniewski et al. ("Drone Detection using Deep
Neural Networks Trained on Pure Synthetic Data", arXiv:2411.09077).
None of these have been deployed inside a closed-loop interception
controller as far as I can tell, and your IBVS+DKF architecture seems
like a natural fit.

Specifically, I would be grateful to know:

1.  Was a real-drone target attempted at any stage, and if so, what
    were the dominant failure modes -- detection, controller, or
    something else?
2.  Do you see fundamental obstacles to substituting a synthetic-data-
    trained drone detector for the balloon detector in your current
    pipeline, or is it primarily a matter of engineering effort?
3.  Are there plans (or already-in-submission work) to extend the
    real-flight experiments to non-cooperative drone targets?

I am exploring this combination as part of a research proposal and
would not want to misrepresent the state of the art or duplicate work
already underway in your group. Any pointers would be very much
appreciated, and I am happy to share what I learn in return.

Thank you for your time, and congratulations on the very nice results
in both papers.
